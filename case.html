Case 01 · Rennie

While the volume of works has sharply decreased across the disclosed tranches, the certified value of each tranche has violently escalated. Of those four numbers, only the third — $22,800,000 — is publicly attributed to CCPERB by name. It is the rare moment the regime publishes one of its own valuations, and it does so because the donor wanted the press release.

Rennie himself estimates the lifetime total of his family's gifts to NGC at approximately $65,000,000, with stated intent to add up to $50,000,000 more by his 75th birthday. The Gallery, asked, declined to confirm.

02 · The numberWhat $22,800,000 of certified cultural property actually is

Cultural-property certification is not a normal charitable receipt. It is the most generous receipt the federal income tax system issues. The stack, per CRA / KPMG / BDO published guidance:

ComponentEffect
Donation tax credit (federal + provincial)≈ 50% of certified value, refundable against income tax
Capital gains on appreciated propertyZero inclusion (vs. 50% normal inclusion)
Annual income limitDeduct against up to 100% of net income (vs. 75% normal)
~77%Effective public subsidy on certified cultural property, top bracket, low cost basis
$17.5MApprox. foregone federal + provincial revenue on the 2025 Rennie gift alone
$0Cash contribution from donor toward shipping or evaluation, beyond what is itself receipted

This is not a hidden subsidy. It is in the act and the bulletins. What is hidden is which works at which prices — the per-certification data that would let the public see how the subsidy is distributed and on what valuations it rests.

03 · The walkFrom an auction price to a certified valuation, step by step

The 2025 Rennie gift contains 40 works by Rodney Graham (1949–2022), the Vancouver-school photo-conceptualist whose lightbox transparencies define the medium. Walk one specific gift through, step by step, and the structural moment becomes visible.

  1. 01 Take a Rodney Graham work that has been to public auction. On 30 May 2018, Oxfordshire Oak, Swalcliffe (1990) sold at Heffel for $175,250 CAD — the highest verified single-work Rodney Graham realization in the Canadian auction record. The lightbox Paradoxical Western Scene (2006) sold at Phillips New York in 2019 for US$56,250.
  2. 02 That is the auction floor. Two strangers, a hammer, no federal tax credit. Anyone — institution, collector, dealer — could have bid. The number is what someone actually paid.
  3. 03 Now look at the 2025 gift. Forty Rodney Graham works, blended into a $22,800,000 CAD certification with twenty-one other works (10 Mona Hatoum, 3 Ai Weiwei, Dan Graham, and others). The per-work breakdown is not published. The total is.
  4. 04 Now do the arithmetic. Assign the entire $22.8M to the forty Graham works — giving zero value to the Hatoums, the Ai Weiweis, the Dan Graham. The implied per-Graham value is $570,000 CAD. That is more than three times the highest verified Graham realization at Heffel, and approximately seven times the confirmed Graham lightbox at Phillips. The Hatoums and Ai Weiweis are not zero. The real implied per-Graham value sits higher than this bound.

03 · The walkHow the closed loop arrives at certification

Audain's case is structurally distinct from Rennie's. The numerical leap between auction floor and certified value — the centre of Case 01 — is mostly not present here: top Riopelle works realize CAD $2.28M to $7.4M at Heffel, and the 2026 MNBAQ gift averages $2.08M per work, in line with the upper auction record. The leap, in this case, is not in the arithmetic. It is in the structure.

  1. 01 The donor donates from his own collection. Audain donated approximately 150 works as the founding gift to the Audain Art Museum, Whistler — a museum he founded in 2016, which bears his name, and whose foundation he chairs.
  2. 02 The recipient institution is one the donor controls or recently chaired. Of the major recipients in the table above, three were chaired or co-founded by Audain himself, one is named for him, and the National Gallery of Canada was chaired by him 2009–2013, with continuing Foundation directorship through 2014.
  3. 03 The valuation appraiser is required to be at arm's length. CCPERB's Directive on Monetary Appraisals requires arm's-length appraisal reports based on relevant sales evidence and market conditions. Above CAD $50,000 per work, two appraisals are required — unless the appraisal is provided by a committee of the Art Dealers Association of Canada (ADAC) or the National Archival Appraisal Board (NAAB), in which case one suffices.
  4. 04 The pool from which arm's-length appraisers are drawn is itself concentrated. The current CCPERB board includes Vancouver dealer Monte Clark, owner of Monte Clark Gallery (founded 1992; currently operating from the basement of Livingspace, the furniture gallery on West 2nd Avenue, after earlier Armoury District premises). Clark is also a past board member of the Art Dealers Association of Canada — the body whose appraisal committee can substitute for two independent appraisals at the $50K+ threshold. He deals in the same Vancouver-school market category that defines the contemporary BC holdings flowing through this regime. The gallery's structural position in the certification regime is institutional rather than commercial: a board seat at CCPERB, ADAC board history, coverage of the relevant market category — none of which requires a flagship retail footprint.
  5. ↓ This is the leap. The "arm's length" requirement is satisfied at every individual node — each appraiser is technically at arm's length from each donor. The architecture as a whole is not. When one figure chairs the foundation that owns the museum that receives his collection, chairs the foundation that holds the work he donates to a different recipient, and previously chaired the board of his other major recipients — the loop is closed without any single arm's-length relationship being violated. Reflexivity is achieved by the structure, not by any particular pair of hands.
  6. 05 The CCPERB tax credit is granted regardless. The disclosure of which appraiser, at what value, on which work, in what fiscal year — is not.

Case 03 · Heffel
$678,500
The only public moment when the regime was tested. The legislative response was to make the regime more permissive, not less. The disclosure question was never reached.

01 · The chronologyWhat happens when someone takes the regime to court

In July 2017 Heffel Fine Art Auction House sold a small Caillebotte oil — Iris bleus, jardin du Petit Gennevilliers (1892), $678,500 to an English gallery — and applied for an export permit. CCPERB refused. What followed is the only public stress-test the regime has ever received. The structural finding is in the timeline of how the federal government chose to respond.

  1. JUL 2017 Sale and refused export. Heffel sells Iris bleus at auction for $678,500 CAD to a UK gallery; applies for cultural-property export permit the next day. CCPERB refuses, declaring the work "outstandingly significant" and of "national importance" to Canada. Only one other Caillebotte exists in any Canadian collection.
  2. JUN 2018 Federal Court quashes CCPERB. Justice Michael Manson rules that CCPERB's interpretation of "national importance" applied to a French painter's work was "unreasonable." Quashes the decision; orders rehearing by a different panel.
  3. MAR 2019 The federal government acts before the appeal. While the Crown's appeal is still pending at the Federal Court of Appeal, the March 2019 federal budget eliminates the "national importance" criterion from the test for tax certification of cultural property. The criterion is retained only for export licences. The legislative change is not contingent on the appeal's outcome.
  4. APR 2019 Federal Court of Appeal reverses. Justice Richard Boivin rules that CCPERB's interpretation should be reviewed on a standard of reasonableness, defers to the board, restores the original refusal. Heffel does not seek leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Canada.
  5. ↓ This is the leap. Two months before the courts settled the question in the regime's favour, the federal government had already legislated. And it had legislated in the direction of removing a constraint on certification, not adding one. The only public test of the regime resolved with the regime's tax-certification scope expanded by act of Parliament. Disclosure of who, what, and how much — was not at issue in the litigation, and was not reached.
In particular, the Board could continue to interpret the "national importance" criterion very broadly such that almost any work can be considered to be of national importance. Heffel Fine Art Auction House, statement on the Federal Court of Appeal decision · 2019

The auction house, in defeat, named the regime's discretionary scope. The government, before the appeal had even been decided, had already moved to widen it on the tax side.

Case 04 · Langmann
$50,000
Not the size of a gift. The threshold above which two appraisals are required — except when the appraisal comes from one specific committee, currently co-chaired by a member of one specific Vancouver gallery family.

01 · The thresholdThe one bypass the regime grants by name

CCPERB requires two arm's-length appraisals for any donation above $50,000 in fair market value. There is exactly one permitted shortcut. The regime states it on its own website:

ADAC is the only fine art appraisal service permitted by CCPERB to prepare one appraisal report for donations over CAD $50,000 in value. Art Dealers Association of Canada · "Learn More About Appraisals" · ad-ac.ca

One Toronto-based industry association — the Art Dealers Association of Canada (ADAC) — is named, by the federal regulator, as the sole body whose single appraisal substitutes for the two-appraiser requirement. The committee inside ADAC that issues those appraisals is the ADAC Appraisal Committee. Above the $50,000 threshold, virtually every certified-cultural-property donation in Canada either goes through two independent appraisers — or through this one committee.

03 · The walkHow the bypass is constructed and where the same family appears on both sides of it

  1. 01 Set the threshold. For a donation valued above $50,000 CAD, CCPERB ordinarily requires two arm's-length appraisals. This is the regime's structural protection against a single appraiser controlling the certified value of a high-value donation.
  2. 02 Permit one substitution. The single shortcut: the ADAC Appraisal Committee's review can substitute for both required appraisals. CCPERB names ADAC, by name, as the only fine art appraisal service permitted to do this.
  3. 03 Identify who runs the committee. The current Co-Chair of the ADAC Appraisal Committee is Jeanette Langmann, Director of Uno Langmann Limited Fine Art (Vancouver, 2117 Granville Street). She is also past President of ADAC and a current ADAC board member. She advises the federal export-review process directly.
  4. 04 Identify the same gallery as a participant in the regime it helps administer. Uno Langmann Limited is also a regulated party of CCPERB. In 2021 the gallery applied to the Canada Border Services Agency for an export permit on a 1946 Emil Nolde watercolour, Seascape with Steamer and Sailboat. The CBSA refused, on the advice of a National Gallery of Canada Expert Examiner. The gallery applied to CCPERB for review. The case is documented as a CCPERB Board Decision in the 2021–22 Annual Report.
  5. ↓ This is the leap. The committee CCPERB names as its only permitted alternative to two arm's-length appraisals is led, in part, by a member of a Vancouver gallery family that is itself a regulated party of CCPERB. The first Vancouver dealer (Monte Clark, Case 02 step 04) sits on the certifying board itself. The second (the Langmanns, here) leads the committee that certifies the substitute. Together they describe both ends of the appraiser-pool architecture the regime depends on the public not seeing.
  6. 05 This is, again, not an allegation. The Langmanns are credentialed art professionals operating well within the published rules, and have been for two generations. The structural fact is the absence of a public registry that would allow the public to read which appraisals from which committees attached to which gifts at which valuations. The Order Paper question turns each of those into a record.

Case 05 · outlier
$1,500,000,000
The anti-pattern. Cases 01–04 all operate inside CCPERB — federal tax expenditure, arm's-length appraisal, charitable certification. This case operates entirely outside it. Included here not as a fifth instance of the pattern, but as its demarcation: a concurrent channel the same Vancouver capital class uses for a different asset class, on a different timescale, under a different legislature, and therefore beyond the reach of the Order Paper question. Its function in this briefing is to sharpen the scope of the question — not to widen it.

02 · The installationFrom private capital to municipal legislature in two election cycles

In 2022, Wilson contributed approximately $380,000 personally, and coordinated further high-value contributions, to Pacific Prosperity Network — a third-party advertising vehicle that went on to be fined by Elections BC for advertising violations targeting left-leaning candidates. PPN sponsored the premiere and panel for the Vancouver Is Dying documentary, which framed the Downtown Eastside as a public-safety crisis requiring abstinence-oriented intervention. PPN also used its Facebook page to raise funds for The Last Door Recovery Society, whose founder publicly characterizes harm reduction as "putting a bandaid on a bullet hole." Last Door's Recovery Capital Conferences have become a cross-provincial hub for conservative abstinence-oriented drug policy announcements.

03 · The walkHow the accumulated parcels became developable, in sequence

  1. 01 Accumulate at commercial-corridor prices. 2012–2013 purchases along the 800-block of East Hastings at $7.25M and $10.7M. The corridor is zoned commercial / SRO. No residential tower pathway yet.
  2. 02 Fund the narrative frame. 2022 election cycle: $380K personal + coordinated contributions to Pacific Prosperity Network; PPN sponsors Vancouver Is Dying, fundraises for The Last Door. DTES residents are reframed from objects of housing policy into objects of treatment and cleanup.
  3. 03 Install the slate. October 2022 — ABC Vancouver sweeps Council, Park Board, and plurality on VSB. Mayor, 7 of 10 Councillors, 6 of 7 Park Commissioners are now from the slate whose narrative-and-funding environment was seeded by PPN.
  4. 04 Harden the narrative into law. Early 2025 — BC's decriminalization pilot rolled back under sustained political pressure consistent with the "recovery industry" framing.
  5. 05 Upzone the corridor. December 9, 2025 — ABC Council passes the DEOD and FC-1 (Thornton Park) upzoning. Inclusionary social-housing requirement cut from 60% → 20%. Maximum height raised to 100 m (~32 storeys). Of the 20% social-housing units, only 20% are at shelter rates — 4% of total units. Land-value multiple on the accumulated parcels follows automatically.
  6. 06 Unlock the larger pool. January 2026 — the draft of the Vancouver Official Development Plan is revised to remove the language prohibiting residential development on school sites. The change is identified publicly by CityHallWatch (Melanie Cheng analysis, March 2026) as a stealth edit between the October 2025 and January 2026 drafts. The ODP is approved at public hearing March 10–11, 2026. The Vancouver School Board, under ABC plurality, is the owner and manager of the parcel pool newly unlocked.
  7. ↓ Where this diverges from Cases 01–04. Cases 01–04 describe a federal tax-expenditure architecture that converts appreciated art into public subsidy and private legitimacy over roughly a forty-year cycle. The present case describes a parallel municipal channel that converts accumulated land into private appreciation over a two-to-four-year cycle, operated by the same class. There is no charitable framing, no arm's-length appraisal, and no CCPERB certification anywhere in the sequence. The subsidy comes directly from zoning uplift on privately-held land, underwritten by the devaluation of social-housing requirements on public-hearing record. The information asymmetry that makes it function is structural: Low Tide's Senior Vice President concurrently chairs the developer-lobby channel (NAIOP Vancouver Government Relations) to the Council that passed the upzoning. This case is listed in this briefing only to make the scope of the Order Paper question legible by contrast. The federal Minister of Canadian Heritage has no jurisdiction over any step of it. A separate instrument, at municipal and provincial level, is what reaches this channel.
  8. 07 This is not an allegation either. Every instrument above is public: campaign-finance filings, Elections BC fine records, Council agendas, public-hearing transcripts, ODP drafts, NAIOP committee rosters. What is missing is the cross-reference that would join them: an itemized mapping from accumulated parcels to the zoning lift they received to the political outlay that produced the policy. That cross-reference is the municipal analogue of the CCPERB transaction register the Order Paper asks for — and it is obtained by the same method, through a different legislature.

→ The pattern
four cases, one outlier
Four surfaces of one architecture. One outlier that runs outside it and sharpens what the disclosure question does and does not reach.

01 · The blackoutWhat the four cases share

The first four cases differ in surface and converge in structure. Rennie shows the leap from market signal to self-ratified valuation. Audain shows the closed loop that achieves reflexivity through institutional architecture rather than through valuation. Heffel shows the regime's adaptive response when tested in court — not contraction, but legislated expansion of permissibility. Langmann shows that the appraiser pool the regime depends on the public not seeing has, in fact, an address: a Vancouver gallery family that holds leadership of the only committee whose single appraisal substitutes for two.

The fifth case, Wilson, does not converge with the first four in structure. It diverges: it operates outside CCPERB entirely, on a different asset class, on a different timescale, and under municipal rather than federal jurisdiction. It is included here to demarcate the perimeter of the question the Order Paper asks — so that "disclose the CCPERB data" is not mistaken for "solve every capital-to-appreciation channel the same class operates." The fifth case is not addressed by this briefing's question. A different instrument, at a different level of government, is what reaches it.

What is common across all four is that the actual mechanics driving the public subsidy remain entirely off the books. The following cannot be obtained by any normal documentary route:

  • The certified fair market value of any individual donated work.
  • The name of the appraiser, or appraisers, supplying any individual valuation submitted to CCPERB.
  • Whether the appraisal was submitted under the ADAC / NAAB single-appraisal exemption (available at the $50,000+ threshold).
  • Whether any current or former CCPERB board member supplied the appraisal — including a board member dealing in the same market category.
  • What the donor originally paid for the work, when, or through which dealers.
  • The appreciation differential — the gap between the donor's cost basis and the certified FMV — which is the actual structural quantity the regime is subsidizing.
  • The proportion of all certifications associated with British Columbia recipient institutions, or with British Columbia donors.
  • The aggregate certified value before and after the March 2019 elimination of the "national importance" criterion for tax certification.

This is not an oversight; it is the design. Each item above is data the federal government already holds. None of it is published. The Order Paper question already in the office turns each one of those items into a matter of public record.

02 · The consequencesWhat is actually being subsidized when the regime is treated as "just fine art"

The defence of the regime is that it is small, niche, cultural — a tax instrument for a tiny class of objects most Canadians will never own or even see. The $17.5M attached to one named gift in Case 01 sounds, in that frame, almost benign. Drawn down to where the funds and the architecture actually flow, six recapture vectors are visible.

  1. Mark-to-market lift on the donor's unsold portfolio

    A CCPERB certification at a specified valuation establishes an appraisal-of-record. That valuation flows downstream as a comparable for the artist's market: the donor's remaining holdings — and the holdings of every other collector of the same artist — become more valuable as a result of the certification itself. The donor is paid twice. Once as a tax credit on the gifted work. Once as appreciation on the held portfolio. The Audain 2026 gift of 37 Riopelles at $76.9M sets a public valuation pole the rest of the international Riopelle market draws against.

  2. Capital-gains avoidance, compounded with the credit

    Certified cultural property is the only major class of donated appreciated property for which capital-gains inclusion is reduced to zero. Pair this with the donation credit and the donor monetizes the full fair market value of an appreciated asset without ever realizing it as taxable income. The longer the holding period and the larger the appreciation, the larger the public's share of the substitution. The regime is, in effect, a wealth-preservation channel for appreciated assets that would otherwise be subject to disposition tax.

  3. Naming and legitimacy on publicly-supported institutions the public never sees the inside of

    The receiving institution is itself, in most cases, partly publicly funded — the National Gallery of Canada (federal Crown corporation), the Vancouver Art Gallery (BC and City of Vancouver capital and operating grants, including a 99-year city land lease at the new building site valued at $100M), the Polygon Gallery (BC capital funding into the rebuilt facility). Naming rights, board representation, and curatorial influence travel with the gift. The donor pays a fraction of the certified value out of post-tax cash and receives, in addition to a public-funded tax credit, permanent identification as the institution's principal benefactor — on infrastructure the public was already funding.

    And the public, having paid for both the credit and the building, mostly does not see the gift. The National Gallery's own page reports roughly 1,200 works on public view at any given time, from a collection of more than 35,000 works of art — about three percent — and that figure excludes the much larger photography (200,000+ items) and prints-and-drawings (27,000+ works) holdings, which are substantially in storage. The Vancouver Art Gallery's permanent collection holds more than 13,000 works; in March 2026 the gallery opened its first dedicated collection floor in more than two decades, displaying just over 200 of them — about 1.5 percent. The remainder lives in environmentally-controlled storage funded by the public. By international peer comparison the Tate displays approximately 20 percent of its holdings, the Louvre 8 percent, the Guggenheim 3 percent. NGC and VAG sit at the bottom of that range.

    The certified gift functions, structurally, as a transfer of ownership into a publicly-funded archive whose holdings are validated by their being there, not by being shown. The certification creates the consensus that the work is canonical. The consensus sustains the certification of the next work. The vault is the chamber in which both happen. The public funds the chamber.

  4. Curatorial capture and market-making for the donor's category

    Donor capital sustains the institutions whose curators select which artists become canonical, which works become authoritative, and which national representations are made. Selection drives valuation. The curator at the Polygon Gallery — an institution Audain co-funded — curated Stan Douglas as Canada's 2022 Venice Biennale representative. International recognition lifts the artist's market. Future certifications of Vancouver-school photo-conceptualism — works held by the same donor class that funded the institutions whose curators drove the recognition — clear higher valuations as a result. The market-making for the donors' own category is paid for, in part, by the public.

  5. Downstream legitimation of upstream BC capital

    In British Columbia, the wealth flowing through cultural-property certification sits structurally downstream of the real-estate sector — the sector the Cullen Commission (2019–2022) documented as the principal corridor in this province for capital flight and proceeds-of-crime laundering. Several of the largest cultural-property donors in the province are themselves principals of major real-estate developers. The Cultural Property regime functions, structurally, as one above-ground federally-certified legitimation channel for wealth generated upstream. The two systems together describe an end-to-end pipeline: capital enters BC through real estate; legitimation completes through art; the public underwrites the legitimation step at approximately 77%.

  6. The intergenerational transfer wave

    The cohort of named major Canadian cultural-property donors is now in active intergenerational transfer. Michael Audain is 88. Bob Rennie has stated his intent to resolve his collection's disposition before he turns 75. The Phil Lind estate is already executing (37 works to AGO, 2024; $1M endowment to Polygon, 2024). The CCPERB pipeline is the most tax-efficient mechanism in Canadian law for moving collections out of estate. The next ten to twenty years of cultural-property certifications will be the single largest wealth-transfer event of this kind in Canadian history. At ~77% effective subsidy, the public is the largest single counterparty to that transfer and has no transaction-level visibility into any of it.

  7. Logistics and storage as the unmonitored shared vault

    Underneath the certifications and the institutional collections sits a private logistics layer — crating, transport, climate-controlled storage, chain-of-custody documentation. In British Columbia the incumbent provider is Denbigh Fine Art Services (Vancouver, founded 1985), as of 2020–21 a subsidiary of the French art-logistics conglomerate Chenue. BC's incumbent high-value art-handling firm is now under European parent control.

    Two facts about how this layer mixes with the public ledger. The Vancouver Art Gallery's own Head of Museum Services, Jenny Wilson, appears in a published testimonial on Denbigh's website characterizing the VAG–Denbigh arrangement as "more than a client relationship, it's a partnership". VAG's own annual reports and audited financial statements do not name Denbigh, do not disclose the contract value, and do not itemize the off-site storage arrangement. The supplier publishes the relationship. The publicly-funded buyer does not.

    Storage need is, simultaneously, the explicit public-record justification for the new VAG building. Materials going back to the 2004 master plan name "more exhibition and storage space" as the primary driver. Current projected cost: $600M (up from $400M), of which approximately $179M is direct public commitment — $100M City of Vancouver land lease at the new Larwill Park site, $50M Province of BC, $25M federal Infrastructure Canada (Green and Inclusive Community Buildings program), $4.3M Department of Canadian Heritage. The public is being asked to fund the building substantially because of storage need. The current solution to that storage need — payments to private logistics firms — does not appear, line-itemed, in the materials supporting the funding request.

    The same private firms typically handle both sides of the eventual donation pipeline — works in transit for private collectors and works on hand for the public institutions those collectors will eventually donate to. A work can sit in private storage on private account for years or decades before the CCPERB certificate is issued and ownership formally transfers to the public institution. This is functionally the structure that, internationally, is operated through freeports (Geneva, Singapore, Luxembourg, Newark) — high-value art held as untaxed, ownership-flexible, collateralizable wealth in long-term storage. Chenue, Denbigh's new parent, operates in that European context.

    The logistics layer is the unmonitored shared vault in which public and private capital take custody of the same physical works on contracts neither party fully publishes. It is, structurally, the bank.

Disclosure of the underlying transactions converts the regime from a niche cultural instrument into what the record shows it actually is: a federally-administered wealth-preservation, market-making, and intergenerational-transfer architecture, attached to the same donor and developer class on whom comparable public-funds questions are already on the record.

Nothing in this briefing claims that disclosure of CCPERB transaction-level data, on its own, dismantles the regime. It does not. What it does is make the regime legible. Once the per-certification data is on the public record — appraiser, value, recipient, geography, exemption usage, fiscal year — downstream actors gain ground they currently lack: the Office of the Auditor General, FINTRAC, public-finance researchers, the press, artist organizations, comparable provincial bodies, and any future committee with jurisdiction over related questions.

03 · The questionHow the Order Paper closes the leap, in the four CCPERB cases

Each clause of the question already in your office is paired to a specific piece of what the four CCPERB cases (01–04) show is currently unknowable. The question does not name any donor or appraiser. It asks for the structural data that would let the public read the regime independently of who is appearing in it. Case 05 (Wilson) is out of scope by design: its architecture operates outside CCPERB and requires a municipal-level instrument distinct from the one this question carries.

  1. Per-certification FMV, recipient, fiscal year · publishes the per-work value distribution inside the Rennie $22.8M, the Audain $76.9M, and every other certification of the past decade.
  2. Appraiser names per certification · names who signed the appraisals.
  3. CCPERB-board-member and gallery-affiliated appraiser frequency · tests the concentrated-pool problem the Audain case names structurally, the current Vancouver-dealer board member illustrates literally, and the Langmann case shows extending into the only permitted bypass committee as well.
  4. ADAC / NAAB single-appraisal exemption usage at $50K+ · shows how often the one-substitutes-for-two pathway has been used, on which gifts, and at what aggregate certified value — the precise mechanism Case 04 names.
  5. BC share of certifications · reveals the Vancouver-collector and Vancouver-appraiser concentration the Rennie / Lind / Audain / Langmann cluster makes structurally visible.
  6. Rationale for non-disclosure · forces the Minister to articulate on the record why a multi-million-dollar public expenditure attached to a single named gift carries less disclosure than a $1,000 election contribution.
Scope This briefing makes no allegation of impropriety against Bob Rennie, Michael Audain, Heffel Fine Art Auction House, Uno Langmann Limited, Jeanette or Uno Langmann personally, the Art Dealers Association of Canada, the National Gallery of Canada, the Musée national des beaux-arts du Québec, the Audain Art Museum, the appraisers involved, or CCPERB — nor against Chip Wilson, Low Tide Properties, Adam Mitchell, Pacific Prosperity Network, ABC Vancouver, The Last Door Recovery Society, the Art Dealers Association of Canada, or any individual officer of these entities. The question is structural and applies regardless of any individual party's bona fides: a federal tax expenditure that subsidizes approximately 77% of a self-selected valuation, from a valuation pool the public cannot identify, with no transaction-level registry. The cases above (01–04) are the largest, most recent, named, and CCPERB-attributed instances currently in the public record. Case 05 is included in its capacity as an outlier — to demarcate what the Order Paper does and does not reach, not to extend the question into channels Parliament is not the correct forum for. The Order Paper question does not require the Minister to investigate any donor or any appraiser. It requires the Minister to publish data the government already holds.
